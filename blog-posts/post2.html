Understanding Antimicrobial Resistance: A Community Workshop Success

Empowering farmers and community health volunteers with knowledge to protect public health

December 10, 2025
Hanan Adan Alio
6 min read
AMR Awareness, Community Health

Community members participating in our AMR awareness workshop in Nairobi's Eastlands area

Last week, GAHI conducted its first community workshop on antimicrobial resistance (AMR) in Nairobi's Eastlands area. The event brought together over 50 farmers, community health volunteers, and local leaders to address one of the most pressing public health challenges of our time.

What is Antimicrobial Resistance?

Antimicrobial resistance occurs when bacteria, viruses, fungi, and parasites change over time and no longer respond to medicines, making infections harder to treat and increasing the risk of disease spread, severe illness, and death. In agricultural communities, misuse of antibiotics in animals contributes significantly to this problem.

The AMR Threat in Kenya

According to the Ministry of Health, antibiotic-resistant infections contribute to approximately 5,000 deaths annually in Kenya. The misuse of antibiotics in both human medicine and agriculture accelerates this crisis, threatening to undo decades of medical progress.

Workshop Highlights

The full-day workshop was designed to be interactive and practical, moving beyond theoretical knowledge to actionable steps that participants could implement in their communities.

52 Participants Trained
85% Knowledge Improvement
30 Community Health Volunteers
22 Farmers & Livestock Keepers

Session 1: Understanding the Basics

Dr. Sarah Kariuki, our guest facilitator from the Ministry of Health, started with the fundamentals: what antibiotics are, how they work, and why resistance develops. Using simple analogies (comparing bacteria to criminals and antibiotics to police), she made complex concepts accessible to all participants.

Session 2: AMR in Agriculture

This session focused specifically on antibiotic use in livestock and poultry farming. We addressed common practices like:

  • Using antibiotics as growth promoters
  • Treating entire herds/flocks when only some animals are sick
  • Using human antibiotics for animals
  • Not completing prescribed treatment courses

Session 3: Practical Alternatives

Instead of just highlighting problems, we focused on solutions. Participants learned about:

  • Good animal husbandry practices to prevent disease
  • Vaccination as a preventive measure
  • Natural remedies for minor infections
  • When to consult a qualified veterinarian

Session 4: Role-Playing Activities

The most engaging part of the workshop was the role-playing scenarios. Participants acted out common situations:

  1. A farmer pressuring a veterinarian to prescribe antibiotics "just in case"
  2. A community member sharing leftover antibiotics with a neighbor
  3. A poultry farmer using antibiotics to promote faster growth
  4. A mother wanting antibiotics for her child's viral infection

Key Takeaways for Farmers

  • Never use antibiotics as growth promoters
  • Always complete the full course of prescribed antibiotics
  • Implement good hygiene and biosecurity measures
  • Work with qualified veterinarians for animal health
  • Practice proper waste management to reduce disease spread

Participant Testimonials

The workshop received overwhelmingly positive feedback. Here's what some participants had to say:

"I've been giving my chickens antibiotics every month thinking it would keep them healthy. Now I understand I was actually making the problem worse. The alternatives we learned today make more sense and will save me money too."
- Joseph Mwangi, Poultry Farmer
"As a community health volunteer, I now have the knowledge to educate my neighbors about proper antibiotic use. The role-playing activities were especially helpful for practicing difficult conversations."
- Grace Akinyi, Community Health Volunteer

Pre- and Post-Workshop Knowledge Assessment

To measure the workshop's effectiveness, we conducted a simple 10-question quiz before and after the training. The results were encouraging:

  • Average pre-workshop score: 42%
  • Average post-workshop score: 85%
  • Biggest improvement: Understanding that antibiotics don't work against viruses (from 25% to 92% correct)
  • 85% of participants reported they would change their antibiotic use practices immediately

Next Steps and Sustainability

This workshop was just the beginning. Each participant received:

  1. A simplified AMR awareness booklet in Swahili
  2. Contact information for local veterinary services
  3. A commitment card with three specific actions to implement
  4. Information about our follow-up support system

We've established a WhatsApp group where participants can ask questions, share experiences, and receive ongoing support. We'll also conduct follow-up visits in three months to assess behavior change and address any challenges.

Expanding Our Reach

Based on the success of this pilot workshop, we're planning to:

  • Conduct similar workshops in 5 additional counties over the next year
  • Develop radio programs in local languages to reach wider audiences
  • Create visual aids (posters, flip charts) for community health volunteers
  • Partner with local schools to integrate AMR awareness into agriculture and health education

How You Can Help Combat AMR

Antimicrobial resistance affects us all. Here's how you can contribute to the solution:

  • Only use antibiotics when prescribed by a qualified professional
  • Never demand antibiotics if your health worker says you don't need them
  • Always follow your health worker's advice when using antibiotics
  • Spread awareness about AMR in your community

If you're interested in hosting an AMR awareness workshop in your community or would like to support our work, please get in touch.
