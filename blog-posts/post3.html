From Waste to Fuel: Our First Briquette Production Trial

Transforming poultry manure and agricultural residues into clean, affordable cooking fuel

December 5, 2025
Mohamed Abass Maalim
7 min read
Waste-to-Energy, Clean Energy, Innovation

Testing our first batch of briquettes made from poultry manure and rice husks

This month, we successfully completed our first trial of briquette production using poultry manure mixed with agricultural residues. The results are promising for creating an affordable, clean alternative fuel source for rural communities while addressing the dual challenges of agricultural waste management and energy poverty.

The Problem: Energy Poverty and Environmental Degradation

In many rural Kenyan communities, families rely on firewood and charcoal for cooking. This contributes to deforestation, indoor air pollution, and significant health risks. Meanwhile, agricultural waste like poultry manure and crop residues are often burned openly or left to decompose, releasing methane and contributing to greenhouse gas emissions.

Our research in Kajiado County revealed that:

  • 85% of households use firewood or charcoal as their primary cooking fuel
  • Families spend 20-30% of their income on cooking fuel
  • Women and children spend 10-15 hours per week collecting firewood
  • Respiratory illnesses from indoor smoke are a leading health concern

Our Solution: Waste-to-Energy Briquettes

By transforming agricultural waste into fuel briquettes, we address multiple challenges simultaneously:

  • Reduces waste disposal problems
  • Provides affordable, clean cooking fuel
  • Creates income opportunities through production and sales
  • Reduces deforestation and indoor air pollution

The Production Process: Step by Step

1

Material Collection

Collecting dry poultry manure and agricultural residues (rice husks, maize cobs, sawdust). Proper drying is essential for efficient combustion.

2

Grinding & Mixing

Grinding materials to uniform size and mixing in optimal ratios. Our trials tested different manure-to-biomass ratios for best results.

3

Binding & Molding

Adding a natural binder (cassava starch) and using manual presses to form consistent briquettes. Simple, locally-made equipment keeps costs low.

4

Drying & Curing

Sun-drying briquettes for 3-5 days to achieve optimal moisture content (below 15%). Proper drying ensures clean burning and high heat output.

Testing Different Formulations

We experimented with several formulations to find the optimal mix. Each batch was tested for burn time, heat output, smoke production, and ease of ignition.

Formulation Burn Time Heat Output Smoke Level Overall Rating
70% manure, 30% rice husks 45 minutes High Low smoke Excellent
60% manure, 40% sawdust 50 minutes Medium-High Very low smoke Very Good
50% manure, 50% maize cobs 40 minutes Medium Moderate smoke Good
80% manure, 20% rice husks 35 minutes Medium High smoke Fair

Test Results and Performance

Optimal Formulation: 70% poultry manure, 30% rice husks
Average Burn Time: 45 minutes per briquette
Heat Output: Comparable to charcoal, higher than firewood
Smoke Production: 70% less than traditional firewood
Ash Content: 15-20% (can be used as fertilizer)
Production Cost: KES 20 per kg (50% cheaper than charcoal)

Field Testing with Community Members

We conducted practical cooking tests with 10 households in Kajiado to gather real-world feedback. Participants used the briquettes for everyday cooking tasks and provided valuable insights.

"I was skeptical at first, but these briquettes cook faster than the firewood I usually use. There's much less smoke, so my eyes don't burn while cooking. The best part is knowing we're not cutting down trees for fuel."
- Naomi Chebet, Kajiado Household

Another participant, Samuel Kiprop, noted that the briquettes produced less ash than charcoal, making cleanup easier. Several households reported that food cooked with the briquettes had a better taste compared to food cooked over smoky firewood.

Economic Analysis and Income Potential

Beyond environmental benefits, briquette production offers significant economic opportunities:

  • Production Cost: KES 20 per kg (including materials, labor, and overhead)
  • Market Price: KES 40-50 per kg (charcoal sells for KES 80-100 per kg)
  • Profit Margin: KES 20-30 per kg
  • Monthly Production Capacity: 500-1000 kg per small production unit
  • Potential Monthly Income: KES 10,000-30,000 per producer

We're developing a business model where youth and women's groups can establish small briquette production enterprises, creating jobs while addressing energy needs in their communities.

Challenges and Solutions

Challenge 1: Odor During Production

Fresh poultry manure has a strong odor that can be off-putting during processing.
Solution: Using properly dried manure significantly reduces odor. Adding aromatic herbs like lemongrass to the mix can also help.

Challenge 2: Seasonal Availability of Materials

Agricultural residues are more abundant after harvest seasons.
Solution: Establishing storage systems and diversifying material sources (combining different types of residues).

Challenge 3: Market Acceptance

Some community members were initially hesitant to use "manure briquettes."
Solution: Demonstration cooking sessions and testimonials from early adopters helped overcome this barrier.

Challenge 4: Equipment Access

Commercial briquette presses are expensive and not readily available.
Solution: We've designed simple manual presses that can be made locally using basic materials and skills.

Environmental Impact Assessment

Our preliminary assessment shows significant environmental benefits:

  • 1 ton of briquettes saves approximately 5-7 trees from being cut for charcoal
  • 70-80% reduction in indoor air pollutants compared to traditional firewood
  • Diverts 1.5 tons of agricultural waste from open burning or landfill per month
  • Carbon-neutral energy source (carbon released equals carbon absorbed during plant growth)

The Road Ahead: Scaling Up

Based on our successful trial, we're planning to:

  1. Establish 5 community briquette production units in Kajiado and Nairobi counties
  2. Train 50 youth and women in briquette production and business management
  3. Develop partnerships with institutions (schools, hospitals) as bulk buyers
  4. Conduct larger-scale field trials with 100+ households
  5. Explore carbon credit opportunities through verified emission reductions

We're also researching value-added products, such as briquettes infused with mosquito-repelling herbs or specially formulated for specific cooking needs (fast-cooking vs. slow-cooking varieties).

This briquette production trial demonstrates that sustainable solutions often come from seeing "waste" as a resource.
