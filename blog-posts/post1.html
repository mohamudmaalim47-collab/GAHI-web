Launching Our Pilot Poultry Waste Management Project in Kiambu County

Transforming agricultural waste into valuable resources for smallholder farmers

December 15, 2025
Osman Adan Abdi
5 min read
Project Updates, Waste Management

Farmers in Kiambu County learning about proper poultry waste management techniques

We're excited to announce the launch of our pilot project for sustainable poultry waste management in Kiambu County. This initiative aims to transform chicken manure from a pollution problem into a valuable resource for smallholder farmers, enhancing soil fertility while reducing environmental contamination.

The Problem: Waste as a Liability

In Kiambu County, poultry farming is a significant economic activity, but it comes with environmental challenges. Each year, thousands of tons of chicken manure are improperly disposed of, leading to water pollution, foul odors, and greenhouse gas emissions. Many farmers see this waste as a liability rather than an opportunity.

Our baseline survey revealed that over 70% of small-scale poultry farmers in the area dispose of manure in ways that harm the environment. Only 15% attempt composting, often using inefficient methods that don't fully utilize the nutrient potential.

Project Objectives

  • 1
    Train 150 farmers on safe manure collection, handling, and composting techniques
  • 2
    Establish 3 community composting centers serving as demonstration and collection points
  • 3
    Distribute free compost to participating farmers for at least one growing season
  • 4
    Reduce improper waste disposal by 60% in target communities within 6 months

Our Approach: The 4-Step Transformation Process

Step 1: Collection and Segregation

We're introducing simple, low-cost collection systems that keep manure dry and prevent nutrient loss. Farmers are trained on proper segregation to avoid contamination with bedding materials or other waste.

Step 2: Aerobic Composting

Using the windrow composting method, we're teaching farmers how to create optimal conditions for microbial breakdown. The key innovation is our locally-adapted compost recipe that combines poultry manure with agricultural residues (like maize stover and rice husks) to achieve the ideal carbon-to-nitrogen ratio.

Step 3: Quality Testing

We're establishing simple field tests to check compost maturity and nutrient content. This ensures farmers receive high-quality compost that won't harm their crops or soil.

Step 4: Application Training

Proper application is crucial. We're training farmers on appropriate rates, timing, and methods for different crops and soil types to maximize benefits.

Early Results and Farmer Testimonials

Although the project is in its early stages, initial feedback from the first 30 trained farmers has been overwhelmingly positive. Mary Wanjiku, a poultry farmer from Githunguri, shared:

"I used to pay someone to take away the chicken manure because it was just causing flies and bad smell. Now I see it as gold for my vegetable garden. The training changed everything for me."

Another farmer, John Kamau, reported that his first batch of compost produced visibly healthier kale seedlings compared to his previous crops grown with chemical fertilizers alone.

Challenges and Solutions

We've encountered some challenges that we're actively addressing:

Challenge 1: Initial resistance from farmers who see composting as too labor-intensive.
Solution: Demonstrating time-efficient techniques and highlighting long-term benefits.

Challenge 2: Limited space for composting in densely populated areas.
Solution: Introducing compact, vertical composting systems that require less footprint.

Challenge 3: Seasonal variations affecting composting speed.
Solution: Adapting techniques to local weather conditions and providing protective covers.

The Road Ahead

Over the next six months, we plan to expand the project to include 120 more farmers and establish two additional composting centers. We're also exploring partnerships with local agricultural extension services to ensure sustainability beyond the pilot phase.

Looking further ahead, we're researching value-added products from composted manure, such as organic fertilizer pellets and liquid plant tonics, which could create additional income streams for participating farmers.

How You Can Support This Project

This pilot project relies on community support and partnerships. You can help by:

  • Sharing our work with potential partners
  • Donating to help us reach more farmers
  • Volunteering your expertise in agriculture or waste management
